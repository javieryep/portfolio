Dabra.ai — upgrading a startup MVP

Role

  • Product, marketing, brand and web design
  • Part of the in-house team in SAP.iO, the internal SAP incubator.
  • Worked alongside a dedicated front-end developer in San Francisco, and with the foundational team based in Israel
  • Duration: 1 year

What is Dabra?

Dabra is a productivity tool designed to facilitate better recurrent one-on-one (1:1) meetings, in order to grow in effectiveness and empathy. How does it work? It’s a mobile-friendly web application that provides a collaborative and private area for setting the meeting agenda; it also suggests previously discussed topics when preparing for an upcoming meeting. Dabra also provides valuable information about colleagues through behavioral insights, which give context for personalized conversations.

Why focus on 1:1 meetings? Because they play a crucial role in the development of workplace relationships, especially between managers and team members. The habit of not preparing for our 1:1 meetings can undermine trust and respect in our workplace relationships.

Two types of users

The relationship between managers and team members is extremely important, which is why they became our main personas.

  • Managers are primarily focused on people and their development. Their goals are to be always ready to make their team members successful in their work.
  • Team members are mostly focused on the projects themselves, and want to have successful relationships with their managers in order to thrive in their careers and get things done at work.

We decided to focus primarily on managers, as they would drive adoption by inviting their team members to Dabra.

The product MVP

Dabra went through a couple of iterations before taking off. The first one was a Slack chatbot that reminded managers to prepare for their 1:1 meetings and keep track of their notes. This iteration didn’t work well, so the product evolved into an email assistant with similar capabilities, but it was not efficient enough for managers to keep track of all meetings notes either. The product evolved once again into a note-taking web application, this time getting tremendous positive user feedback.

The product MVP was developed with a basic interface built using Quasar—an open-source framework powered by the Vue framework. The UI element used to give structure to the content was cards, which worked as containers for different information levels. Meeting cards (1st level), contained topic cards (2nd level), which also contained note cards (3rd level).

The MVP was partially effective, but it had multiple issues as well. The interaction users loved and that took center stage was revisiting topics. This interaction consisted in typing a topic and receiving suggestions of previously discussed topics. This saved users time to go through their notes to find what had been previously discussed. The MVP was also designed with some wrong assumptions. The expectation was that users would mainly take notes on their phones during meetings. After diving into the usage statistics,

the data revealed Dabra was used ~85% of the time on desktop, and only ~15% on mobile.

The card structure of the application had very generous spacing between notes and buttons—for fat thumbs using mobile devices. Users complained about having to look at their screens too often during their meetings to scroll through a few notes.

Taking it to the next level

My role in the team as the only designer was to address the main UX issues, in order to increase the user adoption inside SAP. To accomplish that, I went through a series of whiteboard sessions and sketches to find a better architecture of the product. In order to create more information density, I decided to change the card-like structure of the UI by removing the meeting and topic containers. The intention was to give the app an ‘open-text’ feel but still using independent input field components. Typeface became an important element in the UI as the big bold titles became the natural transition between meetings.

We also encountered new and interesting use cases. One of the most important was the need for an audit trail of specific topic notes, to be used in performance reviews. For this reason, we introduced the notes history view, which consisted in a timeline of notes added to a recurrent topic throughout all the meetings. This became one of the product’s hottest features.

Results

After the release of the product redesign, the number of weekly active recurrent users (WARU) increased by 4 times in just 3 months, a growth ratio that exceeded our expectations. Multiple high level executives started adopting and relying on Dabra for their daily meetings, which increased the visibility of the product inside and outside SAP.

I demoed Dabra during our annual Development Kick Off in the Bay Area, where I showed many SAP employees the benefits of prepared meeting agendas. SAP’s Chief Innovation Officer Max Wessel also demoed Dabra during the annual customer conference that year.

Happy branding

The Dabra mark was initially based on the chatbot idea. It was a friendly-looking green robot, that the team referred to as a ‘personal assistant’. But this concept didn’t fit the latest product iteration, so the brand needed to evolve, as well as the brand language and communication.

The new logo I proposed carried the same personality as the previous one, but it was more abstracted and communicated the emotion we wanted to convey (a happy face) rather than the medium (a chatbot). Visually, the colon idea came from the way users usually abbreviate their one-on-one meetings subjects; this means using the colon in between the attendees’ names.

Along with Dabra’s new logo, I contributed with multiple marketing assets such as illustrations, social media posts, presentations, etc. Finally I also redesigned the product website in order to accomodate a FAQ section, and a knowledge base as well.

Some key learnings

  • Working remotely requires extra effort to communicate clearly. I learned to setting intentional time to explain my thought process.
  • Having a dedicated front-end developer helped me learn alot about how to develop responsive web applications more efficiently.
  • As part of a fast-paced young startup, I had to get creative to research and test my designs by reaching out to those whose profile would fit our manager persona for direct feedback.
  • As the only designer in the team, I became the spokesperson of the product and brand inside SAP and our external users.
