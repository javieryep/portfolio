Cyclical — inventing and patenting a physical product

Never-ending paper waste

Digital applications haven’t replaced paper calendars; they now coexist with them. More over, contrary to what most people would believe, the production and sale of some types is growing. From 2014-15 to 2015-16, decorative and other types of calendars increased by 8 percent to $65 million.

Display calendars combine aesthetics and functionality. As they are highly customizable, business owners use them as effective promotional materials. Customers get them for free and keep them because of their functionality. Even in the era of digital applications, if you want to know the date, lifting your eyes from the screen to a wall that displays a calendar is much easier than opening yet another tab in the browser, or an app in the phone. For practical, but also aesthetical reasons, the use of physical objects instead of digital applications is a growing trend in younger generations.

Perpetually flawed

Perpetual calendars address the waste of excessive paper calendars and the loss of the physical interaction that digital calendars cause. They can be updated to display the current date, hence its name ‘perpetual’, which means they can be purchased at any time of the year, not only the beginning. Despite these benefits, the best-selling perpetual calendars on popular retail websites reveal similar design flaws.

For instance, most of them need to be updated everyday, creating a new daily task. Some types of perpetual calendars comprise several loose pieces, which create the risk of rendering the whole calendar useless if only one of them is lost. Many also are laid out in unclear formats, which complicates their use, especially if someone needs information at a glance. Finally, some perpetual calendars display unnecessary information on most of their surface, showing the relevant information in a reduced space, generating visual noise and distracting the user.

The wheel shaped calendar (1) displays unnecessary information, while relevant information appears in a small area. Both the flip calendar (2) and the cube calendar (3) need to be updated daily. The MoMA calendar (4) is stylistic but impractical due to its small characters and odd form.

The only calendar you'll ever need

What if there was an easier and more enjoyable solution? Cyclical is a perpetual month display calendar that helps reduce the paper waste of disposable calendars and preserving the physical experience people cherish. Cyclical comprises three main pieces that are assembled together to display the information of a full month.

Cyclical solves the main flaws found in common perpetual calendars. For instance:

  • the unnecessary information is hidden, which means that all the information displayed on the calendar is useful,
  • the calendar layout is the same as common calendars, making it clear and simple to read,
  • it only comprises three large pieces that are assembled together, which prevents the risk of losing one of them,
  • it needs to be updated only once a month, thus avoiding a daily task,
  • the replaceable back cover can perform multiple functions such as be a photo frame or cork-board, and
  • it is easier and more enjoyable to manipulate.

The system used in this particular wooden calendar can also be applied using different materials and sizes, allowing for variability in the calendar usage.

A cyclical process

Designing this calendar was not as straight forward as it might look like. It required many hours of sketches, paper prototypes and production. To get the final wooden prototype done, I had to go through four previous iterations, that failed to pass my quality standards. Despite the mistakes, I enjoyed every second of the process to get the calendar right, especially using several power tools, artistic tools, and creating the stop motion video.
