Cyclical — inventing and patenting a product

Background

When I was a senior in college, my wife and I had just moved to a tiny apartment in San Jose, and started decorating the space. We wanted a nice and useful calendar, but we couldn’t find any that fit our desires. So I thought I’d take that as a personal design challenge.

The initial idea

I looked up creative calendars and I found tons of fancy looking, “designy” calendars. The most interesting were the ones that used the same grid and content for all the months, so I came up with my own version. Here it goes:

I overlapped all the months of the year into a single grid. I was pretty proud of that because that’s something I had never seen before, but when I showed it to Kate, she wasn’t thrilled.

“Can’t you hide the things we don’t need? It’s hard to read"—she said.

She was spot on. I had thought something similar but I was enamoured with my initial idea and I didn’t do anything about it. Without her comment I wouldn’t have come up with my invention at all.

Scrappy prototyping

This time, I started looking for something more practical, not only good-looking. I printed my design on paper and started thinking outside the screen. How can I hide the unnecessary information? I folded the days of the week on the left and right forming a loop, but wasn’t as easy with the months. And then it hit me. I could use the same loop concept for the months, but with a different piece of paper.

I came up with multiple versions of that idea on paper, and they all looked promising. It seemed like an obvious solution, it just shifts the grid structure of the days of the month horizontally. I created small, medium and large versions of the prototype and thought of selling the template online for a couple of bucks. Kate even helped me create a tutorial video on how to make the calendar.

Improving the experience

This concept allowed the calendar to be reused every month and every year, but paper is not a very durable material which went against the very goal the calendar.

What's the point of a resuable calendar if you can’t really reuse it for too long?

I decided to take the project futher and prototype the idea in wood. I laser engraved a new design in plywood and cut the pieces individually with an electric saw. Then, I drilled holes for the elastic bands to connect the pieces, but the they didn’t align. Next time, I tried glueing 3 thinner layers of wood for every piece, and leaving gaps in the middle, which became perfectly aligned holes.

This version worked much better, but I still had the problem of displaying unnecessary numbers in some months (29, 30, 31). I realized I could make those specific numbers rotate on the vertical axis to hide the numbers, which were very fiddly and intricate to make, but they worked!

The final aspect of this calendar that bothered me was the back of it, because it doesn’t show anything useful, just random numbers. If the calendar is placed where both sides can be seen, the back looks like a mistake. I decided to use the top holes to hook on a simple cover. That simple back cover evolved into a marketing opportunity.

It works!

When I finished making the calendar I realized I had made something pretty original and useful. Here are some of the main benefits of the calendar:

  • First of all, it’s reusable. That means you don’t need to waste more paper in paper calendars.
  • It can be bought at anytime of the year, not only during the beginning of the year as regular calendars.
  • It’s simple to use, and it only has 3 pieces. Other perpetual calendars have tens of pieces, which is harder to handle and store.
  • The calendar displays a full month’s information, not only one day, as most perpetual calendars.

Can I patent this?

That’s what I asked myself when I realized the uniqueness of my creation. “But I don’t know anything about patents, and I’m sure I wouldn’t be able to afford it at this time.” Before giving up, I started reading a bunch of utility patents. After that I thought to myself

“This doesn’t seem that difficult after all, I think I can do it myself.”

I decided to start the process on my own until I needed professional help. Turns out, I did the whole thing without a lawyer. This was a long and complicated process, and I’ll be describing it in more detail in a Medium article I’m preparing. Stay tuned!

Results

An innovative physical perpetual calendar, patented without a lawyer.

Next steps

One of these days, you’ll be able to order this calendar online. For now, I’m still figuring out how to make that happen. I’ll probably start my own Kickstarter campaign! If you have an idea or someone who might be able to help me, let me know!
